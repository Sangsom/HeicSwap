HeicSwap — what could sink this

HeicSwap · 2026-06-19 · ios-product-team · Reviewed: Discovery + PRD draft v0.9 + Design Spec (Warm Darkroom)

7/10
A strong, data-grounded concept with smart pricing and a genuinely differentiated look — but not yet build-ready. Two issues are business-critical: the free tier is shaped so the mainstream user never pays, and a privacy-branded app is quietly shipping third-party analytics with a “we collect nothing” claim. Fix the free-gate and the privacy-stack contradiction and this is an 8.5.

Per the Execution Pivot, I am not flagging “a competitor already ships this” or “it’s not defensible” — those are normal. Every issue below is about winnability honesty, scope, monetization, feasibility, or accuracy.

Critical Issues — must fix before building

1. The free tier is calibrated so your primary persona never pays

What Free = ~10 conversions/day. But the “Blocked Sender” job is “convert 3–12 photos, once.” That fits under the daily cap — and the cap resets daily, so even a repeat user (8 today, 5 tomorrow) is perpetually free. The only people who hit the wall are big-batch power users converting >10 at once — i.e. exactly the single-use cohort that then churns. You monetize a narrow, churn-prone slice.
Why This quietly undercuts the entire revenue model. With a 10/day cap, free→paid could land well below the 3.5% target, and the payers you do get are the ones the Strategist already worried would churn.
Fix Gate on the value moment, not a daily count: free = unlimited basic HEIC/PNG→JPG at small batch sizes; Pro-gate what people will pay for in the moment — large batch (>N at once), image→PDF, resize-to-target, RAW/WebP, strip-metadata. This is PRD Open-Question Q1 — but it’s not an open question, it’s the linchpin. Resolve it before anything else.

2. A privacy-brand app running Firebase + a “we collect nothing” claim

What The whole wedge is honesty-about-privacy, and Settings says “nothing is collected.” But the PRD specs Firebase Analytics + Crashlytics, which collect device/usage identifiers. The privacy nutrition label would then be inaccurate, and the Settings copy is simply false.
Why This is the most on-brand way to get destroyed — a 1-star review or a tech blogger noticing “the ‘private’ converter phones home to Google” erases the exact trust you’re selling. It’s also an App Store privacy-label accuracy risk and an EU GDPR consent burden (you’re an EU dev).
Fix Pick one and commit: (a) drop third-party analytics, use Apple-only/on-device signals (or none) and keep “we collect nothing” literally true — the cleanest brand play; or (b) keep lightweight analytics but change the copy to the precise truth (“Your photos and files are never collected or uploaded. We use anonymous usage counts to improve the app.”) and ship a proper consent flow. Do not ship Firebase + “collect nothing.”

3. v1 front-loads the riskiest features for the smallest audience

What The “must-have” MVP includes RAW, WebP encode, and a Share Extension — the three biggest complexity bombs, all serving the secondary prosumer persona, not the mainstream Blocked Sender.
Why Each is a schedule killer for a solo dev in 4–6 weeks: RAW (CIRAWFilter) is memory-heavy and slow at the promised 100+ batch (jetsam risk); WebP encode likely needs a libwebp C dependency (build/size/maintenance cost; PRD Q2); and app extensions have hard ~tens-of-MB memory limits — a heavy batch/RAW conversion inside the share extension will crash on real devices. These are the 20% that eats 80% of the time.
Fix Ship the mainstream core in v1: HEIC/PNG/JPG ⇄ + image→PDF + resize-to-target + strip-metadata + one-tap batch. Move RAW, WebP-encode, and the Share Extension to v1.1. The head keywords and the primary persona are fully served without them, and the schedule de-risks dramatically.

4. “Nothing ever leaves your iPhone / works on a plane” breaks on iCloud-optimized photos

What With “Optimize iPhone Storage” on (the default for many), the full-res original lives in iCloud, not on device. Converting it requires downloading from iCloud first — which needs network. The offline promise fails, and there’s a silent fetch the UX doesn’t account for.
Why It’s a real UX dead-end (user on a plane taps Convert → nothing happens / spinner) and a marketing-accuracy problem on a claim that is the brand.
Fix Make the copy precisely true: lead with “Never uploaded · no server conversion” (always true) rather than “never leaves your phone / works offline” (conditionally false). Handle the iCloud case with an explicit “Downloading from iCloud…” state. Downloading ≠ uploading, so privacy is intact — but say it accurately.

5. A $1.99/week sub on a trivial converter — review risk + thin MRR

What Two problems with the weekly: (1) App Review increasingly scrutinizes short-cycle subscriptions on simple utilities for “ongoing value”; (2) the rational single-use user subscribes weekly, does the job, cancels — so the weekly becomes a de-facto $1.99 one-and-done that cannibalizes annual/lifetime and leaves MRR thin.
Why You may be building MRR projections on a tier that mostly produces one-time $1.99 payments and could draw rejection scrutiny on an app whose brand is “no weekly trap” anyway.
Fix Strongly consider dropping the weekly entirely — annual $9.99 + lifetime $19.99 only. That turns “no weekly subscriptions, ever” into a literal paywall headline (weaponizing the #1 category complaint) and sidesteps the review risk. If you keep weekly, model revenue as mostly one-and-done and confirm it survives App Review.

Major Concerns

ASO sequence leads with the term you can’t win yet

Risk Medium
What The metadata leads on “image converter” (diff 45), owned by the two ~17k incumbents. You won’t crack its top-3 at launch.
Fix Win the low-difficulty tail first — “heic to jpg” (15), “convert image to pdf” (23) — in subtitle/keywords; let “image converter” come as authority builds. Keep it in the name field, but don’t expect early rank there.

Your winnability is real but time-boxed

Risk Medium
What The wedge is execution, and an incumbent with 17k reviews (handyCloset/Float) could bolt on an “on-device” badge and a cheap tier in a sprint, neutralizing the ASO message with a review-count advantage you can’t match. (Not a defensibility complaint — a timing one.)
Fix Treat the first 90 days as a land-grab: aggressive review-prompt timing (after a successful batch), fast iteration, and ASA on the tail terms the moment organic traction shows.

The 25% 30-day repeat KPI fights your own thesis

Risk Medium
What You’ve argued this is largely single-use, then set a 25% 30-day repeat target. For a converter that’s likely optimistic and will read as “failure” when it shouldn’t.
Fix Make the primary KPI revenue-per-install (or free→paid), not retention. Track repeat usage as a directional signal, target ~12–15%, and stop optimizing for retention on a utility.

Minor Observations

  1. Temp-file hygiene — converting 100 RAW/large images creates big temp files; spec an explicit cleanup or you’ll fill users’ storage. (Folds into the RAW deferral.)
  2. Brand vs scope — “HeicSwap” still reads HEIC-only while the money is in PDF/resize; the Strategist flagged it, the metadata fixes it — just don’t let marketing drift back to “HEIC converter.”
  3. Support strategy — “Contact” in Settings needs a real destination (email/form) and a refund-handling stance, given utility refund rates.
  4. Serif Dynamic Type — verify .serif titles don’t clip at accessibility XXL sizes in the dark mockups.

What's Actually Good

Credit where due — be specific
  1. The hybrid pricing genuinely answers the churn objection. Annual + $19.99 lifetime monetizes single-use users once instead of pretending they’ll sustain MRR — and the lifetime-above-annual logic is correct.
  2. The teardown is data-grounded, not vibes. “No whale; the two leaders are cloud-based or ad-supported; the private apps have no traction” is a real, exploitable read of the shelf.
  3. Warm Darkroom is a true screenshot differentiator, and the “developing” reveal is on-brand and cheap to build — rare combination.
  4. The honest paywall (no fake countdowns) aligns brand and UX instead of fighting it — most utilities get this exactly backwards.
  5. Anti-goals are disciplined — no cloud, no ads, no editing. The scope is pointed in the right direction even where v1 is overloaded.

Recommended Next Steps

  1. Redesign the free/Pro split (Critical 1) — value-gate, not daily count. This is the single highest-leverage decision; do it first.
  2. Resolve the privacy stack (Critical 2) — decide analytics-or-not, then make the privacy copy and nutrition label exactly true.
  3. Re-cut the MVP scope (Critical 3) — RAW / WebP-encode / share-extension → v1.1; lock a leaner, shippable v1.
  4. Fix the privacy/offline copy (Critical 4) — “never uploaded · no server conversion” + an iCloud-download state.
  5. Decide the weekly’s fate (Critical 5) — ideally drop it and headline “no weekly subscriptions, ever.”
  6. Reset KPIs & ASO sequence — revenue-per-install over retention; win the tail keywords first.
